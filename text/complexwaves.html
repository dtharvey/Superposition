When we add together two sine waves their sum is a periodic wave that retains the smooth shape of a sine wave. If we add together three or more sine waves we can generate more angular periodic waveforms—such as square waves, triangular waves, and sawtooth waves—that are defined by line segments.

The dashed line shows two cycles of a square wave. Describe the waveform’s shape, focusing on ways in which it is similar to and different from a sine wave. Repeat for the triangular wave and the sawtooth wave.

Exercise 3. Return to the square wave and note that there is an equation shown below the figure that shows the summation of \(k\) individual sine waves, where \(k\) varies from 1 to \(\infty\). This summation is called a Fourier series. The amplitude of each sine wave in this series is the product of two terms: the term \(\frac{4}{\pi}\) that sits outside of the summation, and the term \(\frac{1}{2k-1}\) that sits within the summation. The periodicity of each sine wave is given by the term \((2k-1)\) included within the \(\sin()\) function. There is no term for adjusting the phase angle of individual sine waves. Click on the radio button to show the individual sine waves and then use the slider to examine, in turn, the first three sine waves k = 1, 2, and 3 in the Fourier series. Explain how the difference in the amplitudes and the periodicities of the first three sine waves are consistent with the equation for a square wave. Repeat for the triangular wave and the sawtooth wave.
